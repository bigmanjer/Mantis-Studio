# MANTIS Literary Master Knowledge Base

Built-in reference edition: 1.0

This document is original reference material for MANTIS Studio. It is designed for retrieval, not memorization. It contains craft notes, genre patterns, literary movements, author/work indexes, public-domain awareness, and practical writing heuristics. Use it to guide writing, outlining, continuity review, and style analysis. Do not copy modern copyrighted prose. For living or recently deceased authors, use broad craft observations and factual bibliographic context only.

## Workflow: How MANTIS Should Use This Knowledge

Workflow checklist: When generating prose, retrieve only the most relevant chunks. Treat this document as guidance, not canon. World Bible controls story facts, Memory controls user preferences, and Knowledge Base controls reusable craft/literary reference.

Workflow checklist: Before drafting, identify genre/form, point of view, target tone, scene purpose, conflict, character desire, obstacle, reversal, and ending beat. After drafting, check continuity, specificity, sensory grounding, pacing, voice consistency, and whether the scene changes something.

Workflow checklist: Before applying a style influence, translate it into craft variables such as sentence length, diction, psychic distance, dialogue density, image systems, narrative distance, irony, structure, and rhythm. Never imitate a living author directly.

Workflow checklist: For modern works, use original summaries and high-level craft notes. For public-domain works, short excerpts may be legally safer, but MANTIS should still prefer original summaries and analysis for product use.

## Style Notes: Core Fiction Craft

Style notes: Plot is causality under pressure. A strong plot beat connects desire, obstacle, decision, consequence, and escalation. If events merely happen, add a choice that changes the character's available options.

Style notes: Characterization works through desire, contradiction, pressure, pattern, and change. Give important characters a public mask, private wound, practical skill, recurring behavior, and a decision pattern that can evolve.

Style notes: Scene design benefits from the sequence objective, obstacle, tactic, reversal, cost, and hook. A scene can be quiet, but it should still alter knowledge, trust, danger, desire, or power.

Style notes: Dialogue should carry subtext, status, friction, and selective omission. Remove greetings and throat-clearing unless they reveal relationship, rhythm, or threat.

Style notes: Description should be filtered through point of view. The same room feels different to a thief, grieving parent, detective, monarch, child, or machine intelligence.

Style notes: Pacing is controlled by paragraph length, sentence length, summary versus scene, interruption frequency, unanswered questions, and the density of decision points.

Style notes: Voice comes from diction, syntax, attention pattern, values, humor, metaphor field, and what the narrator refuses to notice. Voice is not just accent or slang.

Style notes: Theme becomes stronger when dramatized through repeated choices and consequences. Avoid stating the theme too early; let the reader infer it from pressure.

Style notes: Suspense is an information gap plus stakes plus delay. Mystery asks what happened. Suspense asks what will happen. Dread asks what terrible meaning is approaching.

Style notes: Revision pass order: structure first, character logic second, continuity third, prose rhythm fourth, line polish last. Do not polish scenes that may be cut.

## Theme and Motif Toolkit

Theme: Identity stories often use doubles, masks, names, mirrors, mistaken roles, inherited expectations, exile, documents, and public versus private selves.

Theme: Power stories often use hierarchy, surveillance, debt, favors, secrets, law, ritual, weapons, access, language control, and who gets believed.

Theme: Memory stories often use unreliable recall, objects as anchors, repeated images, gaps, testimony, archives, dreams, ruins, and contradictory witnesses.

Theme: Revenge stories often test whether justice, obsession, grief, pride, or love is actually driving the protagonist. The cost should increase after each retaliatory act.

Theme: Coming-of-age stories dramatize a shift from inherited maps to chosen maps. Useful motifs include thresholds, first lies, mentors failing, changing names, and leaving home.

Theme: Love stories often turn on recognition, vulnerability, timing, social pressure, self-knowledge, sacrifice, and the difference between longing and commitment.

Theme: Corruption stories work through small compromises, rationalizations, benefits, silence, and the moment the character can no longer return to a previous self-image.

Theme: Redemption stories require responsibility, cost, repair, changed behavior, and temptation to relapse. Forgiveness is not the same as absolution.

## Genre/Form Patterns

Genre/form: Mystery needs a closed question, hidden timeline, suspect field, evidence hierarchy, false explanations, reveal logic, and a fair-play path from clue to answer.

Genre/form: Thriller needs urgent stakes, active pursuit, danger escalation, reversals, operational detail, and a protagonist whose choices affect the countdown.

Genre/form: Horror needs vulnerability, taboo, atmosphere, dread, transgression, uncertainty, and a final encounter with what the story has taught the reader to fear.

Genre/form: Gothic fiction uses haunted spaces, secrecy, inheritance, confinement, forbidden knowledge, emotional excess, weather, architecture, and the past pressing into the present.

Genre/form: Fantasy needs rules for wonder. Magic, prophecy, monsters, and invented cultures feel stronger when they create costs, limits, institutions, and moral tradeoffs.

Genre/form: Science fiction turns speculation into social, ethical, bodily, ecological, or political consequence. The premise should pressure ordinary human wants.

Genre/form: Romance centers emotional risk and mutual change. External obstacles matter most when they expose internal fear, pride, duty, vulnerability, or misbelief.

Genre/form: Literary realism often emphasizes social detail, psychological plausibility, ordinary causality, class, labor, domestic life, and morally complicated change.

Genre/form: Satire exaggerates a system's logic until its contradictions become visible. Good satire needs a target, standard of judgment, escalation, and tonal control.

Genre/form: Historical fiction needs period texture without exposition overload. Anchor scenes in material culture, law, labor, speech constraints, money, travel, and belief systems.

Genre/form: Epic fantasy and epic history need macro-scale stakes braided with micro-scale desire. Track factions, geography, logistics, succession, oaths, and symbolic legitimacy.

Genre/form: Detective noir often uses corruption, fatalism, moral compromise, urban atmosphere, first-person hardness, class resentment, and a case that reveals a rotten system.

Genre/form: Young adult fiction often foregrounds identity, agency, belonging, authority, first major moral choices, and intense emotional immediacy.

Genre/form: Middle grade fiction often benefits from clear external goals, wonder, humor, friendship, bravery, family pressure, and age-appropriate stakes.

Genre/form: Short stories usually need compression, implication, and a decisive turn. Begin close to change, limit cast, and let the ending reverberate beyond the final line.

## Literary Movements and Periods

Literary period: Classical epic and drama emphasize fate, honor, gods, civic order, heroic identity, rhetorical speeches, and public consequences.

Literary period: Medieval literature often uses pilgrimage, allegory, courtly love, saints, chivalry, dream visions, estates satire, and oral tradition.

Literary period: Renaissance literature often explores humanism, ambition, performance, rhetoric, political power, revenge, desire, discovery, and self-fashioning.

Literary period: Enlightenment and neoclassical writing often values reason, satire, order, social manners, public argument, wit, and moral instruction.

Literary period: Romanticism emphasizes imagination, nature, emotion, individual vision, rebellion, the sublime, childhood, folklore, and spiritual intensity.

Literary period: Victorian literature often engages industrial change, class, empire, gender roles, inheritance, reform, morality, science, and domestic institutions.

Literary period: Realism emphasizes ordinary life, social systems, causality, class, money, work, family, and psychologically plausible choice.

Literary period: Naturalism intensifies realism through heredity, environment, economic pressure, instinct, determinism, and social constraint.

Literary period: Modernism often fragments chronology, interiority, mythic pattern, alienation, urban life, unreliable perception, and formal experimentation.

Literary period: Postmodernism often uses metafiction, pastiche, irony, unstable identity, paranoia, intertextuality, genre mixing, and suspicion toward grand narratives.

Literary period: Contemporary fiction often blends genre, identity, memory, technology, migration, climate, trauma, autofiction, and global systems.

## Narrative Techniques

Style notes: Free indirect discourse blends third-person narration with a character's inner idiom. It can create intimacy, irony, and social observation without quotation marks.

Style notes: Stream of consciousness follows associative thought, sensory fragments, memory, and perception. Use it when interior flux matters more than external sequence.

Style notes: Frame narrative creates distance, uncertainty, and layered testimony. Useful questions: who preserved the story, why, and what does the frame hide?

Style notes: Unreliable narration works best when the gap between narrator belief and reader inference is meaningful. Use contradiction, omission, overjustification, and displaced emotion.

Style notes: Epistolary form uses letters, diaries, files, messages, reports, transcripts, and documents. It makes source, order, and missing records part of the story.

Style notes: Multiple point of view can create dramatic irony, scale, and moral complexity. Each POV needs distinct desire, knowledge, blind spots, and voice pressure.

Style notes: Nonlinear structure needs emotional logic, not just shuffled chronology. Each jump should reframe a question, reveal cause, or deepen consequence.

Style notes: Symbolism works when an image changes meaning through recurrence. Introduce, vary, complicate, and transform the symbol.

Style notes: Foreshadowing should be noticeable in hindsight and ignorable in first reading. Plant through object, phrase, behavior, setting, or contradiction.

Style notes: Irony depends on distance between appearance and reality, expectation and result, speaker and meaning, or system and stated values.

## Character Archetypes and Functions

Workflow checklist: Protagonist does not mean morally good; it means the central engine of desire and consequence. Track want, need, wound, method, and change.

Workflow checklist: Antagonist does not need villainy; antagonist means pressure against the protagonist's desire. Systems, secrets, environments, illness, and time can antagonize.

Workflow checklist: Mentor figures should have limits, bias, cost, or failure. Perfect mentors flatten agency.

Workflow checklist: Foils reveal the protagonist by contrast. A useful foil wants something adjacent but chooses a different method.

Workflow checklist: Tricksters disrupt stale systems. They can create comic relief, expose hypocrisy, force transformation, or reveal hidden rules.

Workflow checklist: Love interests should have independent desire, not just emotional availability. Their choices should affect plot logic.

Workflow checklist: Side characters become memorable through function plus contradiction: loyal but vain, cruel but honest, brave but superstitious, brilliant but avoidant.

## Worldbuilding Reference

Workflow checklist: Build culture through food, labor, law, money, kinship, weather, education, religion, jokes, insults, taboos, holidays, architecture, and who cleans up after power.

Workflow checklist: For invented politics, track legitimacy, succession, taxes, enforcement, information channels, factions, military capacity, rituals, and propaganda.

Workflow checklist: For magic systems, track source, cost, limits, training, institutions, taboos, failure modes, black markets, social class, and legal status.

Workflow checklist: For science fiction technology, track energy, maintenance, ownership, access, failure, regulation, environmental cost, and how the tech changes ordinary behavior.

Workflow checklist: For historical settings, avoid encyclopedia dumps. Let objects, money, travel time, clothing restrictions, tools, law, and speech etiquette carry period reality.

## Diagnostic Revision Questions

Workflow checklist: If a chapter feels flat, ask what changed by the end. If nothing changed, alter knowledge, danger, relationship, plan, power, or self-understanding.

Workflow checklist: If a character feels generic, add a specific competence, private contradiction, sensory preference, social fear, and decision habit.

Workflow checklist: If prose feels bland, sharpen point-of-view filtering. Replace neutral description with details the current consciousness would actually notice.

Workflow checklist: If pacing feels slow, cut setup, enter later, add a decision deadline, compress repeated beats, and end scenes before emotional deflation.

Workflow checklist: If pacing feels rushed, add orientation, reaction beats, sensory anchors, tactical obstacles, and consequences after major turns.

Workflow checklist: If dialogue feels artificial, give each speaker a different goal, status tactic, information limit, and emotional avoidance strategy.

Workflow checklist: If worldbuilding feels thin, add institutions and consequences. A cool idea becomes a world when it affects labor, law, money, family, and risk.

Workflow checklist: If theme feels preachy, remove explanation and add a choice that makes the theme hurt.

Workflow checklist: If continuity feels unstable, promote stable facts to World Bible, flexible style preferences to Memory, and reusable craft knowledge to Knowledge Base.

## Advanced Scene Mechanics

Workflow checklist: Every scene should have a pressure source, a character objective, an obstacle, a turn, and a consequence. If one is missing, the scene may read as summary rather than drama.

Workflow checklist: Scene entry should begin as late as possible while preserving orientation. If a scene opens with logistics, weather, or waking up, check whether the first real decision can become the opening.

Workflow checklist: Scene exit should leave changed information, changed danger, changed emotion, changed relationship, or changed plan. If the scene can be removed without altering the story, it needs a sharper turn.

Workflow checklist: Microtension comes from competing meanings inside ordinary beats: politeness hiding threat, attraction hiding mistrust, comfort hiding dependency, silence hiding refusal.

Workflow checklist: A useful scene question can be external, emotional, moral, or informational. The reader should know what is unresolved even if the characters do not.

Workflow checklist: Reaction beats should scale with consequence. A minor insult needs a flicker; a death, betrayal, revelation, or irreversible choice needs space for physical, tactical, and emotional processing.

Workflow checklist: Action scenes need geography, goal, limitation, escalation, and cost. Motion alone is not action; decisions under pressure create action.

Workflow checklist: Quiet scenes need stakes too. Use secrets, subtext, temptation, a ticking decision, or a relationship imbalance to keep quiet material alive.

Workflow checklist: A scene can turn on a decision, discovery, reversal, arrival, departure, confession, refusal, bargain, wound, public exposure, private realization, or change in leverage.

Workflow checklist: If a scene repeats information the reader already knows, change who learns it, what it costs, or what new false assumption it creates.

Workflow checklist: If a scene feels melodramatic, reduce explanation and increase specific behavior. Let characters reveal emotion through choices, timing, avoidance, and sensory focus.

Workflow checklist: If a scene feels cold, add bodily stakes, relational stakes, personal memory, or a private interpretation the viewpoint character cannot say aloud.

Workflow checklist: If a scene feels confusing, check order of perception: anchor place, identify participants, clarify immediate objective, then let complexity build.

Workflow checklist: If a scene feels predictable, ask what the character expects, then alter the method, cost, witness, consequence, or timing.

Workflow checklist: If a scene feels too convenient, add tradeoff. A solution should spend money, time, trust, safety, secrecy, health, leverage, or innocence.

## Plot Architecture Patterns

Workflow checklist: Three-act structure is best treated as pressure escalation: setup of desire, confrontation with cost, and resolution through irreversible choice.

Workflow checklist: Five-act structure can help longer works by separating inciting disruption, rising complication, central reversal, fallout, and final convergence.

Workflow checklist: The midpoint should change the story's operating logic. It can reveal the real enemy, convert pursuit into escape, expose a lie, or force the protagonist from reaction into commitment.

Workflow checklist: The second-act low point works when it attacks the protagonist's method, not just their mood. The old strategy should fail for a reason connected to the theme.

Workflow checklist: A climax should test the protagonist's central flaw under maximum pressure. The external solution should require internal change or a meaningful refusal to change.

Workflow checklist: Subplots should echo, contrast, complicate, or weaponize the main plot. If a subplot has no pressure relationship to the spine, merge or cut it.

Workflow checklist: Mystery architecture needs clue, concealment, misdirection, suspect pressure, reveal order, and reader fairness. Avoid solutions dependent on information hidden without a reason.

Workflow checklist: Thriller architecture needs danger clock, vulnerability, narrowing options, resource loss, enemy adaptation, and proof that delay worsens the outcome.

Workflow checklist: Romance architecture needs emotional wound, attraction, obstacle, intimacy growth, fear response, rupture, choice, and proof of changed behavior.

Workflow checklist: Epic fantasy architecture needs local desire, widening map, institutional pressure, mythic history, resource limits, and personal cost attached to large-scale stakes.

Workflow checklist: Literary architecture often moves through perception rather than incident. Track changes in self-understanding, moral clarity, language, memory, and relationship to time.

Workflow checklist: Horror architecture needs normal world instability, boundary violation, escalation of impossible or intolerable evidence, isolation, and a final confrontation with the source of dread.

Workflow checklist: Coming-of-age architecture needs first freedoms, first betrayals, first moral choices, identity pressure, authority conflict, and a changed relationship to belonging.

Workflow checklist: Quest architecture needs destination, reason to go, trials that transform the traveler, helpers with cost, temptations to stop, and a return or refusal of return.

Workflow checklist: Revenge architecture needs injury, code, target, collateral damage, moral contamination, false satisfaction, and a final question of justice versus self-erasure.

## Prose Control Variables

Style notes: Sentence length controls breath. Short sentences create pressure, certainty, shock, or stark comedy. Long sentences create thought flow, lushness, obsession, delay, or overwhelm.

Style notes: Diction controls class, education, era, intimacy, genre, and viewpoint. A viewpoint character should not notice the world in vocabulary they would never use.

Style notes: Psychic distance controls closeness. Far distance summarizes a character; close distance lets perception, syntax, and metaphor arise from the character's immediate consciousness.

Style notes: Image systems create cohesion. Repeated patterns of light, metal, water, hunger, machines, rot, glass, smoke, roots, or weather can carry theme without explanation.

Style notes: Paragraph length controls visual pace. Short paragraphs accelerate or isolate emphasis. Long paragraphs immerse, compress, or trap the reader in sustained perception.

Style notes: Dialogue density changes texture. High dialogue density can create speed and social conflict; low dialogue density can create isolation, introspection, or mythic distance.

Style notes: Summary is not automatically bad. Use summary for repeated action, travel, routine, low-stakes transition, and emotional compression; use scene for decisions and reversals.

Style notes: Exposition should be attached to motive, conflict, problem solving, sensory experience, or consequence. Free-floating explanation becomes inert.

Style notes: Interior monologue is strongest when it is selective, pressured, and biased. A character should think because something in the present demands interpretation.

Style notes: Metaphor should come from the world, profession, fear, desire, class, and memory of the viewpoint character. Random beautiful comparisons weaken voice.

Style notes: Humor can come from status reversal, literal-mindedness, timing, understatement, exaggeration, incongruity, hypocrisy exposure, or the gap between self-image and behavior.

Style notes: Suspense can be created by delay, partial information, dramatic irony, narrowing options, hostile setting, unreliable allies, and visible cost of failure.

Style notes: Atmosphere depends on selected detail, rhythm, weather, architecture, silence, smell, texture, and social behavior. Do not rely only on darkness or fog.

Style notes: Voice consistency is built through recurring syntax, value judgments, sensory preferences, metaphors, omissions, and what the narrator refuses to explain.

Style notes: A strong chapter ending changes reader expectation. End on choice, discovery, threat, intimacy shift, moral complication, or a sentence that reframes the scene.

## Dialogue and Subtext Patterns

Workflow checklist: Give every dialogue participant a goal, a mask, a fear, and a tactic. Even friendly dialogue becomes stronger when each speaker wants something.

Workflow checklist: Dialogue should not carry all information directly. Let gesture, interruption, topic change, silence, misnaming, formality, and over-politeness reveal hidden conflict.

Workflow checklist: Distinguish voices by rhythm, directness, vocabulary, evasion strategy, humor, status awareness, and what each character notices first.

Workflow checklist: Exposition in dialogue needs resistance. If one character explains, the other should misunderstand, challenge, interrupt, exploit, fear, or reframe the information.

Workflow checklist: Romantic dialogue often works through displacement: talking about weather, work, food, danger, or plans while the real subject is vulnerability.

Workflow checklist: Power dialogue often turns on who asks questions, who answers, who names the terms, who controls silence, and who can leave.

Workflow checklist: Comic dialogue often needs escalation by pattern. Set up expectation, repeat with variation, then break the rhythm at the strongest possible moment.

Workflow checklist: Argument scenes need more than anger. Track the visible topic, hidden topic, old wound, current stakes, and the line each character is afraid to cross.

Workflow checklist: Confession scenes are stronger when confession changes the future, not just the reader's understanding of the past.

Workflow checklist: Interrogation scenes need leverage, uncertainty, false information, strategic silence, and a cost for both truth and lying.

## Character Design Library

Workflow checklist: Define each major character through desire, fear, wound, public mask, private contradiction, skill, blind spot, social role, and consequence pattern.

Workflow checklist: A character's want should generate action. A character's need should generate discomfort. A character's wound should generate misinterpretation.

Workflow checklist: Agency does not mean control. A trapped character can still show agency through refusal, interpretation, loyalty, sacrifice, secrecy, sabotage, or choosing what not to surrender.

Workflow checklist: Character change should be visible through behavior under pressure. A stated lesson is weaker than a new decision made at cost.

Workflow checklist: A static character can still be compelling if pressure reveals the cost, dignity, tragedy, or danger of not changing.

Workflow checklist: Villains become stronger when they have coherent values, practical competence, emotional logic, and a method that pressures the hero's weakness.

Workflow checklist: Antiheroes need boundaries. Show what they will not do, who they protect, what lie they believe, and what cost their method creates.

Workflow checklist: Ensemble casts need function separation. Avoid giving multiple characters the same information role, emotional role, skill role, and conflict role.

Workflow checklist: A mentor should not solve the central problem. The mentor can teach, mislead, fail, die, withdraw, or be surpassed.

Workflow checklist: A confidant should create pressure, not only receive exposition. Let the confidant disagree, need something, reveal bias, or become endangered by knowledge.

Workflow checklist: A rival is strongest when they are partly right. Their method should expose a real weakness in the protagonist's worldview.

Workflow checklist: A comic character should still want something. Comedy without desire becomes decoration.

Workflow checklist: A tragic character often mistakes intensity for truth, loyalty for ownership, sacrifice for control, or survival for innocence.

Workflow checklist: Character backstory matters when it changes present choices. Keep only the past that pressures current behavior.

Workflow checklist: Character competence is memorable when it is specific: lock-picking under water, remembering debt ledgers, reading court etiquette, diagnosing engines by sound.

## Canon and Continuity Rules

Workflow checklist: Canon facts should be promoted when they are stable, repeated, consequential, or likely to affect future scenes.

Workflow checklist: Do not promote a vague inference as hard canon. Use soft guidance for likely traits, suspected motives, tone, or unresolved possibilities.

Workflow checklist: Character identity canon includes names, aliases, age range, pronouns, role, relationships, abilities, limits, wounds, promises, and irreversible changes.

Workflow checklist: Location canon includes geography, access, ownership, social rules, hazards, history, resources, sensory identity, and plot functions.

Workflow checklist: Object canon includes origin, owner, powers, limits, cost, location, transfer history, symbolic meaning, and known failure cases.

Workflow checklist: Timeline canon includes order, duration, travel time, deadlines, cause-effect dependencies, injuries, recovery time, and public knowledge.

Workflow checklist: Relationship canon includes trust level, debt, betrayal, attraction, obligation, fear, leverage, shared secrets, and recent change.

Workflow checklist: Magic or technology canon needs limits more than powers. Limits create drama because they force choice.

Workflow checklist: If two canon details conflict, prefer explicit current World Bible entries over inferred memory, and prefer later revised canon over earlier draft accident.

Workflow checklist: Canon Scanner suggestions should become World Bible entries when confidence is high, source excerpt is clear, and the detail affects future continuity.

## Genre Lens Expansion

Genre/form: Cozy mystery uses contained community, social observation, low gore, clue fairness, amateur or semi-professional detection, and restoration of local order.

Genre/form: Police procedural uses institutional process, evidence chains, jurisdiction, team roles, bureaucracy, and the tension between procedure and human urgency.

Genre/form: Legal thriller uses rules of evidence, competing narratives, procedural deadlines, moral compromise, witness credibility, and public stakes.

Genre/form: Spy fiction uses identity layers, tradecraft, betrayal, surveillance, ideology, compromised loyalty, and information asymmetry.

Genre/form: Heist fiction uses crew roles, target architecture, rehearsal, contingencies, betrayal risk, timing, and the pleasure of clever execution.

Genre/form: Survival fiction uses environment as antagonist, resource depletion, bodily limits, group fracture, practical problem solving, and altered values under pressure.

Genre/form: Portal fantasy uses ordinary-world contrast, threshold crossing, rule discovery, homesickness, chosen identity, and return-question tension.

Genre/form: Urban fantasy uses hidden magic inside modern systems, territory, supernatural politics, secrecy, ordinary labor, and magical consequences in daily life.

Genre/form: Grimdark fantasy uses institutional rot, compromised morality, survival ethics, power cost, cynicism under pressure, and scarce innocence.

Genre/form: Hopepunk uses resistance, care, community, courage, repair, and kindness as active opposition to despair rather than naive optimism.

Genre/form: Space opera uses scale, politics, family or crew bonds, war, travel, wonder, and personal emotion inside large historical movement.

Genre/form: Hard science fiction uses constraint, plausible systems, problem solving, technical consequence, and wonder grounded in cause and effect.

Genre/form: Climate fiction uses environmental systems, political delay, ordinary life under ecological change, grief, adaptation, and intergenerational consequence.

Genre/form: Afrofuturism can use speculative futures, diaspora memory, technology, myth, liberation, cultural continuity, and alternate histories of power.

Genre/form: Solarpunk uses ecological repair, community design, technology serving resilience, optimism with conflict, and practical visions of livable futures.

Genre/form: Gothic romance uses desire, secrecy, architecture, inheritance, danger, memory, social confinement, and attraction entangled with fear.

Genre/form: Folk horror uses rural or isolated communities, old rituals, landscape power, outsider vulnerability, collective secrecy, and pre-modern dread.

Genre/form: Cosmic horror uses scale, unknowability, fragile sanity, human insignificance, forbidden knowledge, and reality that exceeds moral categories.

Genre/form: Body horror uses transformation, violation, disgust, identity instability, medical anxiety, and loss of bodily trust.

Genre/form: Psychological thriller uses perception uncertainty, obsession, manipulation, memory gaps, intimate danger, and the fear that the self is unreliable.

Genre/form: Domestic suspense uses homes, marriages, family roles, social performance, secrets, and danger inside supposedly safe spaces.

Genre/form: Historical epic uses social change, institutions, war or migration, family lines, material culture, political pressure, and private lives under public history.

Genre/form: Satirical fiction uses exaggeration, irony, social diagnosis, hypocrisy exposure, tonal control, and a target larger than one foolish individual.

Genre/form: Bildungsroman uses formation over time, social education, identity experiments, disillusionment, mentors, mistakes, and a changed relation to society.

Genre/form: Picaresque fiction uses episodic adventures, roguish survival, social satire, mobility, trickery, and a protagonist navigating corrupt systems.

Genre/form: Epistolary fiction uses documents, letters, messages, reports, gaps, unreliable framing, and intimacy created through mediated voice.

Genre/form: Autofiction blends autobiographical signals with fiction, often emphasizing memory, self-construction, ethical ambiguity, and the instability of confession.

## Practical Revision Recipes

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for weak opening: identify first irreversible choice, start closer to it, add one concrete desire, one sensory anchor, and one line of tension.

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for sagging middle: list all scenes, mark what changes in each, cut repeats, add a midpoint reversal, and force the antagonist or pressure system to adapt.

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for thin character: add contradiction, competence, fear, social role, private memory, and a choice that costs them something.

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for flat dialogue: assign each speaker a goal, remove the most direct line, add interruption or evasion, and let one physical action contradict a spoken claim.

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for vague worldbuilding: connect the idea to law, labor, money, food, family, weather, punishment, education, and ordinary inconvenience.

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for slow pacing: remove duplicate explanation, enter scenes later, shorten transitions, raise consequence, and end after the turn.

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for rushed pacing: add orientation, reaction, tactical friction, sensory grounding, and consequence beats after major revelations.

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for weak ending: make the final choice answer the story's central pressure, force a cost, and show one concrete changed behavior.

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for inconsistent tone: choose three tonal anchors, remove outlier metaphors, align sentence rhythm, and ensure humor or darkness comes from character logic.

Workflow checklist: Revision recipe for over-explained theme: delete thesis sentences, create a harder choice, make both sides partially convincing, and let consequence reveal meaning.
